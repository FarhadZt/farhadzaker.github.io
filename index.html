About

Howdy! I'm a Ph.D. candidate in Mechanical Engineering at Texas A&M University, working at the intersection of electrochemistry, materials characterization, and molecular-scale modeling. My research develops hybrid thermogalvanic systems that harvest low-grade waste heat into electricity — from organic–inorganic electrolytes that push thermopower well above the conventional ceiling, to the first Portland-cement thermogalvanic cells that generate power and retain load-bearing strength.

Across my Ph.D. and earlier work I've contributed to 25 peer-reviewed publications (1,400+ citations, h-index 18, i10-index 20) and 2 U.S. provisional patents, spanning batteries, composites, ion-selective membranes, and sustainable cementitious materials. I'm broadly interested in any place chemistry and materials meet energy efficiency, and I'm graduating in May 2027, looking for industry materials/R&D scientist roles.

Published as M. Zakertabrizi; I go by Farhad Zaker.

Research

My work develops materials and electrochemical systems for harvesting low-grade thermal energy and improving battery electrodes. Three current threads:

Thermogalvanic energy harvesting

Hybrid organic–inorganic electrolytes that synergize Fe(CN)63−/4− with alkaline-stabilized semiquinone redox chemistry, reaching 36 mV K−1 Seebeck (~28× the conventional Fe(CN)6 baseline) and ~15% of the Carnot efficiency at a 10 K gradient.

Thermogalvanic cement (dissertation)

The first Portland-cement thermogalvanic cell — designing pore architecture to balance thermo-electrochemical performance against mechanical integrity, so a load-bearing wall can also harvest waste heat.

Battery electrode architectures

Nanopatterned LiFePO4 cathodes produced by spray deposition, where a geometric shape factor links LFP–rGO ratio to the cohesion–performance trade-off (+14% capacity, 4× structural cohesion vs. slurried baseline).

Molecular-scale simulation

MD/DFT modeling of MOF-on-MOF ion-selective cavities (K+/Li+ selectivity 84, Na+/Li+ 80), graphene-oxide–cement interfaces, and orbital-overlap friction in woven nanofabrics.
